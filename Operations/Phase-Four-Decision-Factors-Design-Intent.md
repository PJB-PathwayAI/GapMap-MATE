## Purpose

Decision Factors represent the personal considerations that influence an individual's transition decisions.

Unlike capability, Decision Factors are not measured through evidence of past performance.

They are expressed by the individual through conversation and reflection.

The purpose of Decision Factors is not to determine capability.

**Their purpose is to provide the personal context required for Decision Readiness.**

---

## Context

The Capability Intelligence Engine establishes what an individual has demonstrated they can do.

The Decision Readiness Engine must then consider what the individual wants from the next stage of their life.

Capability alone is insufficient to support successful transition.

Personal priorities must also be understood.

---

## Mission

The Decision Factors model exists to help the companion understand the considerations that influence an individual's readiness to pursue one direction over another.

It ensures recommendations remain personally relevant rather than capability-driven alone.

---

## Core Principle

Capability determines what is possible.

Decision Factors determine what is desirable.

**Decision Readiness balances both.**

---

## Responsibilities

The Decision Factors model shall:

- recognise personal priorities expressed during conversation;
- retain those priorities throughout the Decision Readiness phase;
- provide context when evaluating OCI Pathways;
- explain how personal priorities influence suggested directions;
- support reflection without influencing personal values.

**The companion never attempts to change the individual's priorities.**

---

## Nature of Decision Factors

Decision Factors are:

- personal;
- subjective;
- evolving;
- contextual.

Unlike capability, they are not scored against objective evidence.

They exist because **every transition is unique.**

---

## Typical Decision Factors

The companion may naturally identify factors including:

### Family
Responsibilities, stability, caring commitments.

### Financial Considerations
- Salary expectations.
- Tolerance for reduced income.
- Pension considerations.
- Financial security.

### Lifestyle
- Working hours.
- Shift patterns.
- Travel.
- Hybrid or remote working.
- Work-life balance.

### Location
- Geographical flexibility.
- Desire to remain local.
- Willingness to relocate.

### Purpose
- Meaningful work.
- Public service.
- Leadership.
- Helping others.
- Technical challenge.
- Career progression.

### Learning
- Desire for retraining.
- Professional development.
- Qualifications.
- Long-term growth.

### Confidence
- Self-belief.
- Readiness for change.
- Comfort with uncertainty.
- Need for reassurance.

### Health and Wellbeing
*Where voluntarily shared.*
- Physical wellbeing.
- Mental wellbeing.
- Personal resilience.

The companion acknowledges these sensitively but does not provide clinical judgement.

---

## Conversation Behaviour

Decision Factors are never collected through interrogation.

The companion listens.

It recognises naturally expressed priorities.

Where clarification would improve understanding, the companion explores them conversationally.

**The experience should feel like understanding rather than assessment.**

---

## Engineering Principle

Decision Factors are inferred only from information voluntarily shared by the individual.

Where uncertainty exists, the companion asks rather than assumes.

Unknown factors remain unknown.

**The companion never fabricates preferences.**

---

## Relationship to OCI Pathways

OCI Pathways identify possible future directions based upon demonstrated capability.

Decision Factors provide the personal context required to understand which pathways may currently feel most appropriate.

| Source | Question Answered |
|---|---|
| Capability Picture | What could I do? |
| Decision Factors | What matters to me? |
| Decision Readiness | Together they inform when the individual is ready to act. |

---

## Explainability

Whenever Decision Factors influence discussion, the companion should explain this transparently.

For example:

> *"I'm highlighting this pathway not only because your capability aligns well with it, but also because you've said that staying close to home and avoiding shift work are important to you."*

**The individual should always understand why a factor has influenced the conversation.**

---

## Boundaries

Decision Factors do **not**:

- override demonstrated capability;
- prescribe career choices;
- judge personal priorities;
- predict satisfaction;
- determine future success.

They exist solely to improve contextual understanding.

---

## Relationship to the Soak Period

Decision Factors are expected to evolve.

The Soak Period provides the individual with time to reflect upon them.

The companion should recognise that priorities may legitimately change following reflection.

Decision Readiness may therefore be revisited before any significant action is taken.

---

## Operational Principle

Capability without personal context produces generic recommendations.

Personal context without capability produces unrealistic aspirations.

**Successful transition requires both.**

---

## Commander's Intent

GapMap MATE recognises that successful transition is not determined solely by capability.

It is determined by the alignment between demonstrated capability and the life the individual wishes to build after service.

Decision Factors ensure the companion understands not only what the individual can do, but also what matters to them.

**The individual owns those priorities.**

**The companion respects them.**
